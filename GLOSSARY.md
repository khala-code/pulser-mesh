# Pulser Mesh Glossary

## Anti-extractive conditions

The initial rules used to reject graph states that look structurally extractive rather than scarcity-reducing. These include bounded growth, minimum social embedding, and mission consistency with measured scarcity.

## Checkpoint

A validation boundary that accepts or rejects a local graph state, computes common surplus, and emits a dividend pulse if the submitted state satisfies protocol conditions.

## Coherence floor (\(\Xi\))

A measure of whether a node is stable enough to maintain identity, commitments, and mission continuity over time. In protocol terms, \(\Xi\) is the threshold below which a node behaves too incoherently to be trusted as a stable participant.

## Common surplus

The validated portion of local economic activity that remains after anti-extractive checks and can be redistributed as a flat dividend rather than privatized by an apex.

## Consent edge

A graph edge representing a favor, transaction, or obligation that is valid only if the involved parties have mutually accepted it. This is a foundational rule of the local mesh.

## Dividend pulse

A downstream distribution of validated common surplus to participating persons. It is called a pulse because it is emitted at checkpoint intervals rather than existing as a permanent wage or centrally managed welfare stream.

## Geographic grounding (\(\vec{g}\))

The location and scale at which a node claims to operate. This can be a neighborhood, watershed, city, region, or broader unit depending on the scarcity class being addressed.

## Heegner checkpoint

A mathematically “clean” validation boundary inspired by class-number-1 ideas: a place where ambiguity is minimized and local state can be compressed into a more orderly form.

## Local mesh

The place-specific graph of people, firms, favors, transactions, care commitments, and transformations from which protocol-valid surplus emerges.

## Mission vector (\(\vec{m}_s\))

The direction in scarcity space that a node claims to move the world. It is not branding language; it must be testable against a real scarcity field in a real geography.

## Node tuple

The working economic fingerprint of a participant:

\[
\mathcal{N} = (p, q, \phi, \vec{m}_s, \vec{g}, \Xi)
\]

This tuple encodes structural embedding, growth, mission, place, and coherence.

## Pulser Mesh

The overall protocol and ecosystem. A geometry-only economic pump that turns local meshes into verified surplus pulses.

## Pulser Protocol

The formal rules, data structures, and validation logic that define how Pulser Mesh works.

## Scarcity field

A measurable field describing the magnitude and structure of a deficit at a location. For food, this typically includes availability, access, utilization, and stability.

## Settlement core

The minimal chain-based or cryptographic layer that finalizes checkpoint acceptance and distributes dividend pulses.

## Shell

Another name for the checkpoint layer. The shell compresses local graph state, much as a new chamber in a shell encodes prior growth.

## T1 / T2 / T3

A three-layer normative distinction used in the project:

- **T1**: individual standing and inalienable rights.
- **T2**: interpersonal and family/kin mesh norms.
- **T3**: impersonal market and firm logic.

The distinction helps prevent market logic from swallowing care networks or personhood itself.

## Transformation operator

An activity or institution that converts inputs into outputs in a way that increases alignment with a real scarcity-deficit vector. Grain to bread is a canonical example of a useful transformation.

## Trompe / pulser pump

A no-moving-parts hydraulic system that uses entrained air, falling water, and pressure differences to lift water above its source. This is the primary image behind Pulser Mesh.

## Trophic efficiency

The fraction of total resource input that becomes human-bioavailable food output. This matters for evaluating whether a food pathway genuinely reduces food scarcity.

## Value-added transformation

A stronger form of “value-add” than standard accounting. In Pulser Mesh, value-added means moving a resource closer to satisfying an actual local scarcity vector.
